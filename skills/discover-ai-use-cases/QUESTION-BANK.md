# Question Bank — eliciting real use cases

The quality of the use cases you find is capped by the quality of your questions. The research is blunt: **leading questions bias answers** — people mirror the interviewer's words and you lose the unexpected insight you were after. And people **cannot name latent needs** directly ("faster horses"); you reach those through *concrete past behavior*, not opinions about the future.

Three disciplines run through every stage:

- **Open, not leading.** Ask for a description, not a yes/no or a pre-loaded answer. ✅ "What's easy or hard about getting your weekly report out?" ❌ "Isn't the weekly report a pain?"
- **Past behavior, not hypotheticals (the Mom Test).** ✅ "Tell me about the last time you did X — what did you actually do?" ❌ "Would you use AI for X?" Hypotheticals get you politeness, not truth.
- **Don't pitch the solution.** Until Stage 4 you never mention AI features. Suggesting the answer contaminates the signal — you'll "discover" the use case you walked in wanting.

Ask a **small batch (3–5)** per stage, then listen and follow the thread. Use the user's own words back to them. One genuine follow-up beats three scripted questions.

---

## Stage 1 — Frame and calibrate

Goal: set the honest frame and get just enough context to steer. Light touch.

- "I'm going to ask about how your week actually goes, then point out a few spots where AI could genuinely help — not generic tips. Sound good?"
- "What's your role, and what would you say you actually spend the most time on?"
- "Roughly how does a normal week split — meetings, focused work, admin, comms?"
- "Do you want a quick pass, or should I dig in deeper where it looks promising?"

Avoid: "What do you want to use AI for?" / "What would you automate if you could?" — both invite stated wants and skip the real routine. You'll get there by Stage 3, grounded in evidence.

## Stage 2 — Map the workweek (day-in-the-life)

Goal: a concrete, chronological list of recurring tasks with rough frequency and time each. Description, not judgment.

- "Walk me through what you did yesterday, start of day onward — actual tasks, not the ideal version."
- "What happens every week without fail? What about every month?"
- "Where do you wait on someone, or hand work off and wait to get it back?"
- "What do you end up doing outside normal hours, or wish you'd gotten to?"
- "Anything boring or fiddly that you didn't mention because it's just 'part of the job'?"

For each task, pin down: **how often** (per day/week/month) and **how long** (minutes/hours per instance). Reflect the list back: "So the recurring ones are A, B, C, D — did I miss any?"

## Stage 3 — Hunt the signals

Goal: find tasks carrying the high-impact markers — *repetitive, tedious/low-value, time-consuming, error-prone, draining/stressful, information-transforming*. Still no AI talk.

Surface the pain (Mom Test — concrete and past):
- "Of everything on that list, which would you happily never do again? Tell me about the last time you did it."
- "When was the last time something took way longer than it should have? What happened?"
- "What do you redo, or copy from one place into another, over and over?"
- "What's the task you keep putting off? What makes it unpleasant?"
- "Where do small mistakes creep in, or where do you double-check yourself?"

Drill to the root (5 Whys): when they name a frustration, ask "why" — up to ~5 times — until you hit the real bottleneck. The fix often attaches to the root, not the symptom.

Find latent needs (normalized pain): people stop noticing chronic friction.
- "What part of the job do you think just *has* to be tedious — that's 'how it is'?"
- "If a sharp assistant started Monday, what's the first thing you'd hand them?"

Tag each task with the markers it carries. **Information-transforming** is easy to miss and high-value — listen for "I take X and turn it into Y" (notes→summary, call→action items, spreadsheet→slides, data→narrative, one format→another).

## Stage 4 — Match (now you may discuss solutions)

Goal: confirm fit before you score. Now AI is on the table — but check reality, don't sell.
- "For [task], what would 'done well' look like? What does the output need to nail?"
- "What inputs does it start from, and are they written down somewhere you could point an AI at?"
- "If something here drafted 80% and you reviewed it, would that help — or is the judgment the whole job?"

That last question separates **automation** (hand it off) from **augmentation** (AI drafts, human decides). Both are valid; naming which one matters for scoring and expectations.

---

### Anti-patterns to catch in yourself

- Asking a yes/no when you want a story.
- Naming the AI feature before the person named the pain.
- Accepting the first surface complaint without a "why."
- Letting them stay abstract ("usually," "in general") — pull them to the last real instance.
- Over-interviewing: if you've got 3–6 strong signals, move on. Discovery has diminishing returns.
